Section 4 · The honest part

The same questions everyone has, answered.

The number one reason people hesitate on this is that the math sounds too good to be true. So let's address that directly. These are the actual objections we hear on every introductory call. The honest answers are below.

"Sounds like a Ponzi scheme."
A Ponzi pays earlier investors with new investor money. This pays you with the actual receivables of a real business. Each week, an ACH pulls money directly from a restaurant, gas station, or service business that owes us. Their daily revenue is the source of your return. You can verify the businesses exist. You can verify the bank statements. Nothing about this depends on bringing in new capital. Public MCA operators like OnDeck show 46% portfolio yields on regulatory filings — the math is consistent with the entire industry, not unique to us.
"Why isn't everyone doing this?"
They are — but at the institutional level. CFG Merchant Solutions secured a $145M credit facility from institutional lenders in 2024 and stopped taking individual investors years ago. American Express paid ~$850M for Kabbage. Private equity owns most of this market. What's rare is access to a smaller, ethical book that's still open to individual lenders. We're capping ours at ~$2M under contract before we close to new participants.
"What if the business doesn't pay?"
We have first-position UCC liens on the business's receivables, equipment, and inventory - so we get paid before anyone else. The contract has a 7-to-30 day window built in so we have room to work. In practice, we deploy $20K to businesses approved for $50K - we never lend to the limit. If they're late by a day, we have multiple paths: redirect receivables, sell inventory, ACH pull. Your capital is the protected position.
"How do I get my money out if I need it?"
Every Friday, your position closes. You decide on the spot: redeploy into next week, or pull it out. There is no multi-month lock-up. If you start with $5K to test, you can pull all $5K plus your return after one week. The redeployment is your choice, not a contractual obligation. (Once we hit our $2M cap and switch to line-of-credit fulfillment, we can pay out same-day on emergencies.)
"This must be predatory to the businesses."
Traditional MCAs are. They charge 30-40% with compounding interest and try to bury businesses to capture assets. We charge a flat fee around half that and we work with businesses when they're late instead of penalizing them. Most of our borrowers come back for repeat funding because the math still beats their other options - bank loans they don't qualify for, or larger MCAs they can't afford.
"Why do you need outside capital at all?"
Right now, we don't have a $30M line of credit. Our short-term plan is to get to $2M under contract from individual lenders, build the track record, and then walk into a bank with that cash flow to secure a line of credit. Once that happens, we don't need new capital, and existing lenders get rolled into a more flexible structure (including same-day liquidity). You're getting in early to a book that closes.

Section 8 · The contract

The agreement, in plain English.

Our contract is fixed and non-negotiable - that's not a sales tactic, it's because every line was drafted by counsel to protect you as the first-position lender. Here's the structural anatomy.

🤝
Parties

You are the lender. Our partner LLC is the borrower (technically - they then place capital with the actual business). The business owes us, and our liens protect the chain.

📐
Position size

Each position is for a fixed dollar amount and a fixed weekly return. No floating rates. No hidden fees. The math is the math, every week.

📅
Term: 7 to 30 days

Standard term is 7 days. The contract allows up to 30 days for repayment edge cases - built-in operational buffer that almost never gets used.

🔒
Collateral

UCC-1 filing on the business's receivables, equipment, and inventory. Liens are in our (and your) favor. We can enforce ACH withdrawals immediately on default.

⚖️
Why it's not "lending"

Legally, this is structured as a purchase of future receivables, not a loan. That's what keeps it outside usury laws and lets the structure work - and it's how every regulated MCA operator does this.

↻
Renewal vs payout

Each Friday, you decide whether to renew (deploy into next week's position) or close (we wire your funds back). Renewal is your election, not an automatic rollover.

Note: Because the contract is lender-protective and modeled on industry-standard private-lending agreements, we don't negotiate line items. The strength of your position depends on the contract not being weakened. Counsel reviewed it both for legal soundness and for your protection - every change request would have to start by re-establishing both.

Quick answers

Frequently asked questions.

What's the minimum deposit?

$5,000. The standard position is $20,000 because the math scales linearly - you earn 20 points on $20K, 15 points on $15K, 10 points on $5K, etc. Most participants start at $5K to test, then scale to $20K within 2-3 weeks once the first payments hit.

Can I deploy more than $20K?

Yes. Larger positions are split across multiple businesses to keep concentration low. A $100K deposit, for example, would typically be deployed across 5+ businesses simultaneously. This is by design - never overweight a single borrower.

How quickly can I get my money out if I need to?

Standard exit is at the end of any week - when your weekly position closes on Friday, you can choose payout instead of redeployment. Once we hit our $2M cap and switch to a line-of-credit fulfillment model, we'll be able to support same-day emergency liquidity. Until then, your maximum wait is one weekly cycle.

Is this an investment, legally?

No - it's structured as a purchase of future receivables, which is the legal frame every regulated MCA operator uses. This keeps the program outside both SEC investment rules and state usury laws. The contract uses "lender" and "borrower" language, never "investor" or "investment." This isn't a workaround; it's the standard structure for the entire MCA industry.

Do I need to be an accredited investor?

No. Because this is private lending - not an investment offering - the accredited-investor rule doesn't apply. That said, we do confirm you understand what you're committing to, and the contract makes the structure explicit.

What happens if a business defaults?

The 7-30 day contract window gives operations room to recover the position before any "default" is even called. We have first-position UCC liens on the receivables, equipment, and inventory - so if recovery isn't happening through normal collection, we have multiple legal paths to make you whole, all of which sit ahead of the business's other creditors. In the program's history, we've never had an investor lose principal.

Is there a tax advantage?

The income generated is taxable as it comes in (receivables income / interest equivalent - your CPA will classify based on your structure). Many participants leave their capital deployed continuously to delay realized tax events as long as possible. We're not your tax advisor - talk to one - but the redeployment optionality lets you actively manage the timing of recognized income.

Why is your program capped at $2M?

Two reasons. First, scaling beyond $2M from individual lenders runs into operational complexity that pushes us into territory better served by institutional credit. Second, our exit plan is to use a track record at $2M to secure a bank line of credit and self-fund. Once that's in place, we don't need new lenders, and existing participants get access to a more flexible structure (including same-day liquidity).

What's the catch?

Honestly? The biggest "catch" is that this kind of structure is uncommon at the individual-lender level - most MCA capital is institutional now. The other catch is that the program is closing once we cap. If you want a position, the time to start the conversation is while we're still open.
